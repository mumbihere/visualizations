Marriage and Women's Independence

Women of different cultures are expected to get married at different points in their lives. In some countries, it is understood that a woman should wait until she is established in the world independently, with life experience all her own. In others, women get married soon after they move out of their parents home as young adults, facing the world with a partner from the start. National statistics paint different life stories of young women around the world.

In the following statistical narrative, we will be using the term independence to indicate a lifestyle that is both largely unsupported financially by others, and without any notable dependents to be supported.

Freedom from Childbearing

Marriage historically and culturally is a structure for bearing and raising children. Children, babies, and pregnancy all limit women’s independence and freedom. In some countries, the average woman has many children, as we saw with Nasra. It could be that there is no access to effective contraception, that these babies are happy accidents. Child mortality rates tell another story, where families may be large to protect from all too common early deaths.

Learning and Labour

Education is often considered to be a proxy for a woman’s independence — oftentimes, with more education comes more career opportunities, and with more career opportunities come more personal financial stability.

When a nation has less than 12 years of average male education, corresponding average female education drops considerably below the equal expectancy line; many of these nations have low average female marriage ages. With 12 or more years of expected male education, many nations tend to approximate the equal school expectancy line, and tend to have higher-than-average female marriage ages. The “School Expectancies” figure suggests that when a country’s focus is on family life and childbearing, and less-so on education, educational inequality considerably increases. Alternatively, when a country seems to value more time inschool, the education emphasis applies to both men and women; women additionally appear to postpone building their families in order to complete this education.

In considering labor participation rates, the average rates for women are (predictably) lower than those for men; the range of rates is also larger in women than in men across different nations. It is notable that the nations with higher-than-average female marriage ages tend to converge near mean labor participation rates, across both genders.
